Key data on wildlife trade

     Network Analysis 2012/2015/2018


Network Analysis
Italy, United States, Switzerland, France are four main countries engaged in the global wildlife trade. While from 2012 to 2018, the importance of Italy decreased. Asian countries such as Singapore, China and South Korea are in the second tier.



Wildlife Imports
Asia stayed on top consistently, engage in importer trade around 6000 times a year during 2012-2016 and 5000 times during 2017-2018. Followed is Europe, whose trading frequency is as two-thirds much as Asia. However, the Africa, Oceania and South America wildlife accounts for little. Furthermore, we could find the frequency of import trades steadily declined yearly.

Wildlife Exports
Europe held the incomparable leadership position on global export trade, exporting more than 7000 times a year. Same as the importer analysis, the Africa, Oceania and South America also exported few compared with other continents. The trend of Europe, Asia and North America’s trade had an evident interannual fluctuation.

Wildlife Origins
This diagram shows the difference of concentration of the origins of wildlife products by continents, which seems like have a similar ranking all these years. North America stayed on top consistently, which reached the peak of 6166 in 2013, while Europe was on the opposite. Furthermore, we could figure out the amount of exporting trades fluctuated down yearly.

Wildlife Source
Note ：C-bred in captivity; D-commercial bred; F-born in captivity but not bred in captivity; I-confiscated or seized; O-pre-convention; R-ranched; W-wild.

Most species being traded were bred in captivity and wild, and these two kinds of source showed a continuous decline during 2012-2018. In contrast, ranched species were gradually traded more, which seemed to be a good trend we would like to see.

Trading Term
Regarding the trading terms, it is important to note that the leather products refer to the processed animal skin. The charts illustrates that large leather products accounted for the most part of trade instead of raw skins. Among leather products, larger ones are more preferred.

The most common animals in the trade


Python Reticulatus

The reticulated python is a snake species in the family Pythonidae native to South and Southeast Asia. It is the world's longest snake and listed as least concern on the IUCN Red List because of its wide distribution. In several range countries, it is hunted for its skin, for use in traditional medicine, and for sale as a pet.

Crocodylus Niloticus

The Crocodylus niloticus is a large crocodilian native to freshwater habitats in Africa. It is widely distributed throughout sub-Saharan Africa, occurring mostly in the central, eastern, and southern regions of the continent, and lives in different types of aquatic environments such as lakes, rivers, swamps, and marshlands.

Bobcat

The bobcat is a medium-sized North American cat that first appeared during the Irvingtonian stage around 1.8 million years ago (AEO). Although bobcats have been hunted extensively by humans, both for sport and fur, their population has proven resilient though declining in some areas.

Loxodonta Africana

The African bush elephant, also known as the African savanna elephant, is the largest living terrestrial animal. Since 2004, it has been listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List. It is threatened foremost by habitat destruction, and in parts of its range also by poaching for meat and ivory.

Hippopotamus Amphibious

The hippopotamus is a large, mostly herbivorous, semiaquatic mammal and ungulate native to sub-Saharan Africa. Hippos inhabit rivers, lakes, and mangrove swamps. Their reproduction and childbirth both occur in water.

Ursus Americanus

American black bear is a medium-sized bear. It is the continent's smallest and most widely distributed bear species. The American black bear is the world's most common bear species. Along with the brown bear, it is one of only two of the eight modern bear species not considered by the IUCN to be globally threatened with extinction.

Equus Zebra Hartmannae

The Hartmann's mountain zebra (Equus zebra hartmannae) is a subspecies of the mountain zebra found in far south-western Angola and western Namibia. Hartmann's mountain zebras prefer to live in small groups of 7-12 individuals. They are agile climbers and are able to live in arid conditions and steep mountainous country.

Panthera Tigris

The tiger is the largest extant cat species and a member of the genus Panthera. It is most recognisable for its dark vertical stripes on orange-brown fur with a lighter underside. Many people in China and other parts of Asia have a belief that various tiger parts have medicinal properties, including as pain killers and aphrodisiacs.
